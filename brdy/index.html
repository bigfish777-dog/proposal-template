My takeaways from the call.

There's a line from the call I keep coming back to. I asked if your five sales guys could tell me who your best customers are, and you said: "They wouldn't have a clue."

You then followed it up with: "I know that every month we're putting on 100 customers. I just need to know who the hell they are."

That tells me most of what I need to know about where the real problem sits.

  • You've tried email outreach. 20,000 emails, multiple sectors, multiple countries. Almost nothing came back.
  • You're running LinkedIn outreach at volume - 5,000 connection requests a month - with modest results.
  • You're about to test paid search but there's a question mark around whether people are even searching for what you sell.
  • And now you're considering Meta and Reddit.

My belief? None of these channels failed because they're the wrong channels. They underperformed because the foundation wasn't there.

There's a real risk of going out to the market without a clear picture of who your best customers are, what triggered them to buy, or how to find more people in the same situation. That's the bit we need to fix first. Everything else gets easier once it's in place.

"Every customer you sign up is a blueprint for the next batch. A wind farm operator buys from you, and there are a finite number of wind farm operators in the UK. The answer isn't mass outreach to thousands. It's targeted campaigns to the specific companies who already have the problem, sector by sector, one vertical at a time."

On the email outreach specifically.

I want to be straight about this, because I know you've been told by other agencies that they can make it work.

The emails themselves weren't great. They were generic, templated, and easy to ignore. That said, I'm not confident that better creative alone would have delivered significantly better results.

The real issue was that you were sending to people who may not even be aware they have a problem that satellite broadband could solve. You were targeting by sector and company size, but that doesn't tell you whether a specific business is struggling with connectivity right now.

A construction company with offices in central London and a construction company building a wind farm on the Aberdeenshire coast are both "construction companies" in Apollo. Only one of them needs you. And even then, the timing has to be right.

The difference isn't just better writing. It's a real deployment, a specific geography, a named application, and a reason to believe we've actually looked at their business. That kind of email gets read. It might not get a response every time, but it doesn't get deleted on autopilot either.

The challenge, of course, is that you can't write emails like this at scale without knowing your customers, your case studies, and your targets inside out. Which brings us back to the foundation.

What I'd do: 90 day plan.

Phase 1 - Weeks 1 to 4
Build the Foundation

Customer intelligence capture. Your sales team sign up 100+ connections a month, but nobody is capturing the data that would tell you who to target next. We build a simple intake form that takes 2 minutes per deal: sector, company size, use case, what triggered the purchase, deal size, decision maker role. Nothing complex. No CRM overhaul. Just a lightweight process that turns every new sale into usable intelligence. Over 90 days, that gives you data on 300+ customers.

Segmentation and ICP development. Using whatever historical data you can pull together combined with the new intelligence coming in, we identify the 3 to 5 sectors where you've got the strongest traction - not just "construction" or "renewables" but specifically: what type of company, at what stage, operating where, buying how many units, through which decision maker.

Case study development. You've got deployments across mining, construction, tourism, maritime, healthcare, offshore energy. Each one is a story that should be making the next prospect in that sector feel like you've done this before. We turn the best 3 to 5 into short, punchy marketing assets: who they are, what the problem was, what you did, what the result was.

Connectivity assessment tool. Rather than trying to sell a product to people who don't know it exists, we position Brdy as the expert advisor. A "connectivity assessment" - either a short online questionnaire or a free advisory call - gives prospects a way in that doesn't require them to already know satellite is the answer. It works as a landing page for paid search, as a CTA in outreach, and as a lead qualification tool all at once.

Website review. Your new B2B site is launching imminently. We review it with fresh eyes and provide recommendations on messaging, conversion paths, and how to integrate the assessment tool.

Phase 2 - Weeks 4 to 8
Go to Market, Sector by Sector

Sector campaign - first vertical. We pick the sector where you've got the strongest proof and the most identifiable targets. We go deep on one sector rather than shallow on five. Using AI, we identify 100 to 200 target companies in that sector, research them individually, find the decision makers, and build a multi-touch outreach campaign that uses your case study for that sector as the proof point.

AI powered prospect research. If you've sold to a tourist rail operator, we can use AI to find every other leisure rail operator in the UK. If you've deployed to wind farms, we can map the operators, find out which sites are in development, identify the people running those projects. The research that would take a person days takes minutes. We then enrich that data: what connectivity they're likely using now, where their sites are, how remote those locations are.

Weekly email cadence. A weekly email that goes out to your growing database of contacts, leads, and prospects. Not a newsletter. Not a corporate update. Short, story-based emails about interesting things happening in your world. A deployment you did last week. A problem you solved in an unusual way. The kind of content that people actually read, even when they're not ready to buy yet.

Meta ads test. We've spent about £8,000 on Meta since January and acquired five new clients from it. The algorithm is remarkably good at finding B2B buyers now, even though people assume Facebook is consumer only. Ad spend starts small (we'd recommend £1,000 to £1,500/month to test) and scales based on results.

Paid search test. A small Google Ads campaign pointing to a dedicated landing page built around the connectivity assessment. Targeting problem-aware search terms: "internet for remote site," "broadband for construction," "wifi for temporary location."

Phase 3 - Weeks 8 to 12
Optimise, Expand, Prepare for Amazon Leo

Campaign analysis and iteration. By week 8, we'll have real data on which outreach is getting responses, which Meta creative is performing, whether paid search is generating quality leads, and which email content is resonating. We optimise: more effort into what's working, less into what isn't.

Second sector campaign. We take the playbook from the first vertical and apply it to the next one. Different sector, different case study, different targets, same methodology. Each sector you crack becomes a repeatable template.

Amazon Leo launch preparation. When Amazon launches its satellite broadband service in the UK, awareness of the entire category spikes. Every business that's been putting up with poor connectivity suddenly realises there's another option. We prepare landing pages, email sequences, content, and outreach specifically designed to capture that wave of interest. The companies that are positioned and ready will win.

Sales enablement. Your business development guys are currently more reactive than proactive. We create the tools they need to follow up effectively: email templates that reference the right case study for each sector, a clear follow-up process for warm leads, and guidance on how to use the case studies and assessment tool in their conversations.
