My takeaways

Georgina,

First off, thanks for giving up your bank holiday Monday. And thanks for not being one of the people who books a call and doesn't show up.

I want to be upfront about something. When you first said "gallery," I wasn't sure we'd be the right fit. We typically work with coaches, consultants, and B2B service businesses. Art galleries aren't our usual world.

But the more you talked, the more I realised this isn't really an art problem. It's a marketing and BD problem. You've got a beautiful two-floor space just off Oxford Street. You've got the kitchen, the flexibility, the full rights to the building. You've got 10 years of exhibitions under your belt and a crowd that turns up.

What you haven't got is someone who wakes up every morning thinking about how to fill your event calendar and get your art in front of the people who actually buy it. Without you having to chase them.

That's something we can fix.

The plan.

You're running three businesses. The gallery is the one that's costing you money, propped up by the postgraduate courses. You want it self-funding. That's the mission.

But the problem isn't the gallery itself. The space is gorgeous, the location is prime, the exhibitions are well attended. You said it yourself: you can get the crowd in. The problem is what happens after they leave, and what's not happening between events.

There are three gaps:

Gap 1: Nobody's doing the follow-up.

Your team enjoys the event, celebrates the success, then moves on. The relationship maintenance falls back on you. Clients drift. The phone stops ringing. You end up picking it all back up because if you don't, nobody does.

You put it perfectly on the call: you don't want to micromanage, but if you don't, things don't work very well. That's an exhausting cycle to be stuck in.

Gap 2: The space isn't being proactively sold.

You've tried platforms. Storefront took 20% of everything and then piled VAT and catering commissions on top. Venue Scanner doesn't respond to enquiries. You've got regular clients, but nobody is actively going out and finding new ones. Fashion houses, cosmetic brands, corporate teams, champagne growers, book publishers. They're all out there. They just don't know your space exists.

Every evening the gallery sits empty is revenue lost. Your hotel analogy is spot on: every bed not slept in is money gone.

Gap 3: The art sales pipeline has stalled.

You know the buyers are out there. Interior decorators, hotel groups, corporate offices. Hundreds of buildings going up, every one of them needing art on the walls. But getting to the decision maker takes time, persistence, and a system. Not a spreadsheet of ideas that nobody's executing.

You said you want to take the gallery to the next level. Triple steps, even. But you can't do that while you're the one chasing leads, managing staff, and trying to grow three businesses at once.

PHASE 1
Workstream 1: Fill the Space (Event Hire)

The goal: Build a proactive system that generates venue hire enquiries without relying on third-party platforms or word of mouth alone.

Target list build

We'll research and compile a list of 50-100 businesses and individuals who should know your space exists. Event planners. Corporate events teams. Fashion and beauty brands looking for launch venues. PR agencies booking space for clients. Interior design firms. Wine and spirits companies who run tastings. We'll find the decision makers, not just the company names.

Outreach campaigns

Multi-touch outreach to the target list. Email sequences, LinkedIn messages, and where it makes sense, physical mail. Something that lands on a desk, not in a spam folder. Persistent without being a pest. Each target gets multiple touches over time until they respond.

Positioning and messaging

Right now, if someone stumbles across the gallery online, do they immediately understand that this is a versatile, two-floor event space with a kitchen, just off Oxford Street, that can host everything from product launches to champagne tastings to corporate team building? Probably not. We'll sharpen the messaging so the space sells itself when people find it.

Past client reactivation

You mentioned that before COVID, things were fantastic. You've still got those contacts. We'll build a simple system to stay in touch with past clients and keep the gallery front of mind. Not a newsletter. Just thoughtful, well-timed outreach that reminds them you exist.

PHASE 2
Workstream 2: Sell the Art

The goal: Get original art in front of the people and businesses who actually buy it.

Dream buyer identification

Interior decorators. Hotel groups. Corporate offices with new builds. Property developers. We'll build a targeted list of businesses that buy or commission art at scale, and identify the person within each one who makes that decision.

Relationship-building outreach

This isn't cold selling. It's opening doors. Invitations to private viewings. Introductions to specific artists whose work fits their projects. Curated emails showcasing new collections. The kind of approach that makes them feel like they've got an inside connection to the West End art scene.

Collectors and private viewings

You already run collectors dinners and private viewings. We'll help you promote these more effectively, grow the invitation list, and make sure every event leads to follow-up conversations, not just a nice evening.

PHASE 3
Supporting Activity

Social media

You've got 12-13,000 followers on Instagram, which is a solid base. We're not a social media management agency, so we won't pretend to be. But what we will do is make sure the content you're putting out actually supports the outreach and relationship-building work. We'll write captions, suggest post ideas, and make sure your social presence tells the right story to the people we're putting the gallery in front of. When an interior designer gets our email and clicks through to your Instagram, it needs to back up everything we've just said.

Weekly emails

We write and send a weekly email for every client we work with. Not a newsletter full of exhibition dates and opening hours. Story-based content that people actually want to read. An anecdote about a collector who walked in looking for one thing and left with something completely different. A behind-the-scenes look at how an artist created a piece. The kind of emails that make someone think 'these people are interesting' rather than reaching for the unsubscribe button. This keeps you visible to past clients, warm prospects, and anyone on your list, every single week, without you having to write a word.

Website review

We'll go through the site and make sure it's doing its job. Clear messaging, easy enquiry process, the space presented properly.

Your investment.

I'll be straight with you. I considered whether our standard pricing made sense here, or whether this needed something different. It doesn't need something different. The scope of work is comparable to what we do for any client at this level.

For context, if the outreach lands you a single corporate venue booking or a single interior design firm buying a collection, you've covered it. And you said yourself, "a hotel bed that goes unslept in is revenue lost". How many empty evenings does the gallery have right now?

And hiring even a junior marketing coordinator in London to do this work would cost you £30-35k a year before you factor in employer's NI, pension, management time, and the fact that they'd still need you telling them what to do every week. Add someone senior enough to actually think strategically and you're north of £70k. And you'd still be managing both of them.

Our retainer works out at roughly £33k a year. For a team that doesn't need managing.
