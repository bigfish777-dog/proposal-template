My take

Gio, I enjoyed the call. Not just because you're the only person who's ever (re)booked a call with me because of my mum, but because you're clearly not someone who needs convincing that marketing matters.

You've been doing it. You've been spending money on it. You've got Google Ads running, outreach happening, telesales going, SEO ticking along, LinkedIn on the go. The effort isn't the issue.

The issue is it's all on you.

You're coordinating all of it (and "badly", as you put it).

But I don't think that's quite right. I think you're coordinating it as well as any business owner could while also running the actual business.

But the result is the same:

  • A load of moving parts that aren't connected
  • No single view of what's working
  • A business that's grown to £450k largely on the back of being good at what you do rather than because the marketing is pulling its weight.

Here's what stuck with me from the call:

You've got a goldmine you're not mining. Eight years, 2,500+ projects, a client base full of developers and architects who keep coming back when they've got a new scheme. But instead of proactively reaching out to them, you're waiting for them to remember you exist.

The fact that they are coming back to you speaks volumes, but it still leaves a decent opportunity to get things moving from the people who've forgotten you exist, but who are still gettings drawings done.

These are people who already trust you, already know the quality, and are working on new projects right now. They just need a nudge.

Your Google traffic is converting, but probably not as well as it could. "Explore how top developers are doubling their sales with our CGIs" is a headline written for you, not for them. I'm pretty confident the developers searching for CGI services aren't looking to "double their sales". They're looking for speed, quality, and someone who won't mess up their marketing images two weeks before launch.

The traffic's coming in. The question is how many of those people are landing on the page and thinking "yes, this is for me" versus bouncing because the messaging doesn't match what's in their head. That's a gap.

You've been burned by "brand awareness" marketing. £18k on Meta, three jobs to show for it, and a guy who told you it was about brand awareness when the numbers didn't add up. That's not brand awareness, that's a 'get out of jail free' card for something that didn't work. We don't operate like that. Everything we do will be measured against one question: did it bring in projects?

The fragmentation is costing you more than you think. PPC guy over here, telesales over there, outreach campaign just starting, LinkedIn dabbling, SEO ticking along. None of it talks to each other. None of it feeds a central system. You're essentially running five separate experiments with no control group and no one joining the dots. That's not a marketing strategy. And it sounds like a fucking headache!

What I'd do

This isn't a 47-page strategy document. It's what we'd actually build in the first 90 days, in priority order, with the stuff most likely to make you money sitting at the top.

Phase 1: Unlock the easy money (Weeks 1-4)

Re-engage your existing client base.

This is the single biggest opportunity I can see. You've got eight years of clients sitting in Pipedrive who haven't heard from you in months. Developers with new schemes in the pipeline. Architects who send you work when they think of you, but don't always think of you.

We'll build a proper reactivation campaign. Not a generic newsletter. Not a "just checking in" email. A structured sequence that reminds them you exist, shows them recent work, and gives them a reason to pick up the phone.

For developers, that might look like: "We noticed [area/region] has had a spike in planning approvals. If you've got a scheme coming through, we can usually get first drafts back within [X] days. Here's what we did for a similar project last month."

For architects, it's different. They need to know you're available and reliable. A monthly touchpoint showing recent work across different project types keeps you front of mind when the next brief lands on their desk.

This is the fastest path to revenue because these people already trust you. They don't need convincing on quality. They just need prompting.

Fix the website conversion path.

The Google traffic is coming in. That's working. What we need to do is make sure the landing page, the messaging, and the enquiry process are converting as many of those visitors into actual leads as possible.

That means testing headlines that speak to what developers actually care about (speed, accuracy, no drama), tightening up the pricing guide download flow, and making sure the follow-up after someone downloads is immediate and personal, not just an auto-email that sits in their inbox.

We'll also look at the SEO content. You've got a solid blog. But most of the posts are written for Google, not for humans who might actually become clients. We can sharpen some of that content so it does double duty: ranks well AND converts readers into enquiries.

Phase 2: Build a direct pipeline to developers (Weeks 3-8)

Direct mail to planning application leads.

You've already got the data. Planning applications tell you exactly who's building, what they're building, and roughly when they'll need marketing images. You're already using this for telesales and email outreach. What you're not doing is standing out.

Every developer with planning approval is getting cold emails. Very few are getting something physical that lands on their desk. We'll design and execute a direct mail campaign that puts your work in front of them in a way that can't be ignored. A well-designed piece showing before/after (plans vs CGI renders) with a clear call to action. Something they keep on their desk, not something they delete from their inbox.

This ties into the email and LinkedIn outreach you've already got running. The physical piece arrives first. Then the digital touchpoints reinforce it. That's a coordinated approach instead of three separate channels hoping to get lucky independently.

Tighten up the enquiry-to-project process.

Right now someone fills out a form, gets a pricing guide, and then it's a quick conversation. That works when the lead is hot. But for the warmer ones, the people who downloaded the guide but didn't follow up, there's no system catching them. We'll build a proper follow-up sequence that nurtures those leads without you having to remember to chase each one manually.

Phase 3: Build the referral engine (Weeks 6-12)

Architect and estate agent partnerships.

This is the longer play, but it's the one that compounds. You told me on the call that a good architect can send you 20-30 projects a year. A good estate agent relationship works the same way. The question isn't whether these relationships are valuable. It's whether you're systematically building them or just hoping they happen.

I mentioned on the call how we built a referral network for a financial advisor. Same principle applies here. We identify the architects, estate agents, and interior designers who are working with the types of developers you want. Then we build a targeted approach to get in front of them: content they'd actually find useful, direct outreach that doesn't feel like spam, and a reason for them to recommend you over whoever they're currently using.

This won't produce results in week one. But by month three, you should have a handful of active referral relationships that are sending work your way regularly. And unlike Google Ads, those relationships don't stop working when you stop paying.

What we're not doing

We're not running Meta ads. You tried it. £18k, three jobs. The audience isn't there for your service at your price point. Developers with planning approval aren't scrolling Instagram looking for a CGI studio. They're on Google or they're asking their architect. We'll focus where the intent is. We can explore this in future when the time's right though.

We're not doing telesales. Four or five jobs in five months from overseas cold calling isn't a strategy. It's a lottery ticket. If the outreach campaign you've just started with Instantly produces results, great. Keep it running. But we won't be building our plan around cold calling as a primary channel.

We're not doing "brand awareness." Everything we do will be measured against revenue. If it doesn't contribute to projects coming through the door, we're not doing it.
